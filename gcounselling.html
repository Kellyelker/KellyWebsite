Group Counselling

During our Group Counselling Course, I developed a manual for group therapy for adolescents struggling with anxiety. As one of my special interests is working with adolescents, I developed a manual using mindfulness and CBT to help reduce adolescent stress and anxiety using a variety of techniques. During my time running a mental health program at St. Mary’s High School, I took part in many group therapy sessions with our students that was led by the program psychologist.

The proposal below provided the rationale for creating the group: Group Therapy for Adolescents Struggling with Anxiety.

“Anxiety disorders are the most commonly diagnosed psychological disorders and mental health concern occurring in 5% to 19% of all adolescents (James, James, Cowdrey, Soler, & Choke, 2018). Berk (2011) states that adolescence is the time period marked by a great deal of transitions from the ages of 13 to 18 years of old. La Greca and Ranta (2015) argue that the transition periods of adolescence include an increase of new social and emotional functioning, however, the novelty brings uncertainty, failure, and distress. High school students, aged 14 to 18, are faced with many concerns and questions regarding their future after high school. Many adolescent students suffer from Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD), which is characterized in the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual (DSM-IV-TR), as excessive worry or anxiety, restlessness, irritability, sleep problems, and difficulty concentrating (American Psychiatric Association, 2013). Furthermore, adolescents that are diagnosed with GAD suffer from chronic, excessive worry in areas such as homework, tests, and assignments, social interactions, difficulty controlling worries, and perfectionistic seeking (Connolly, Suarez, & Sylvester, 2011). Adolescents with an anxiety disorders are more likely to also suffer from depression, eating disorders, attention deficit disorders (Kumara & Kumar, 2016). If adolescents are left untreated, they may face numerous risks such as, poorer academic results, avoidance of social activities, and substance abuse problems (Kumara & Kumar, 2016).

Discussion Board Post from Group Counselling Class, EDPS 626.

I explored the process of debriefing in counselling. Throughout my time working as a teacher and counsellor, I have found the process of debriefing a necessary and critical element to grounding myself and being able to leave my day behind me when I go home.

“A sparkling moment was reading about debriefing as a crucial element in group therapy in chapter 11. Kottler & Englar-Carlson (2015) argued that regular debriefing should be scheduled after every group session. The benefit of debriefing right after a session is that the coleaders can critique each other’s performances, giving each other constructive feedback and can also discuss and analyze the group dynamics and possible goals for each member (Kottler-Carlson, 2015). I co-ran a mental health program with another teacher, two educational assistants, a social worker, and a psychologist and everyday after school we would meet for at least 15 minutes to go over the day and discuss the students. I found that after an emotional day, I needed to debrief before I went home. On days were perhaps we didn’t debrief I would go home and sometimes cry and watch television – definitely not a good idea. I also found that coaching after school or working out helped me end that part of the day and start a new part. Overall, debriefing is a critical component in many fields including education, health care, and those who are first responders (Sawyer, Eppich, Brett-Fleegler, Grant, & Cheng, 2016). Sawyer et al., (2016) list a number of methods on how to debrief which I found very interesting to read since there are so many ways we can use debriefing as a tool in group therapy.”

Kottler, J. A., & Englar-Carlson, M. (2015). Learning group leadership: An experiential approach (3rd ed.). Thousand Oaks, CA: Sage.

Sawyer, T., Eppich, W., Brett-Fleegler, M., Grant, V., & Cheng, A. (2016). More than one way to debrief: A critical review of healthcare simulation debriefing methods. Society for simulation in healthcare, 11(3). DOI: 10.1097/SIH.0000000000000148
