Research & Assessment

Research Methodology in Counselling

This course allowed for the exploration of the methods and processes for designing and conducting research in practical settings. One of the main take-aways from this course was understanding different purposes and assumptions of the qualitative and quantitative paradigms of research. Our group designed a quantitative study to test whether a mindfulness-based intervention for adolescents would increase resilience and decrease emotional distress.

During my practicum, I implemented mindfulness-based cognitive behavioural therapy interventions on a number of clients. I enjoyed combining two interventions I have used on students in the past and have enjoyed continuing my learning on combining these two interventions. Below is an excerpt from my Final Case Conceptualization, EDPS 644 where I describe MCBT.

“Implementing the combination of mindfulness and cognitive behavioural therapy aims to teach individuals to identify and gain control over maladaptive cognitive processes with the goal of rewiring brain patterns with positive and realistic ones (Cayoun, Francis & Shires, 2019). Mindfulness in conjunction with CBT has been shown to be effective in treating depression (Segal, Williams, & Teasdale, 2002) and anxiety disorders (Roemer & Orsillo, 2002). Mindfulness-based cognitive behavioural therapy was initially developed for individuals suffering from depression as a relapse prevention treatment and now has been adapted to treat individuals with a range of mental health conditions (Alsubaie, et al., 2017). Alsubaie et al. (2017) argue that MCBT decreases a variety of health and mental health concerns, such as anxiety, depression, coronary heart disease, and diabetes.”

Assessment Theories and Practices

This course provided the opportunity to enhance the role of assessment, screening, and progress monitoring. Listed below, from the EDPS 616 course outline, are counselling related concerns in the field of assessment that counsellors must learn to become competent in.

“1. Professional assessment must always be client-centered, intended to better understand the person, including strengths and needs, in the context of their socio- cultural environment and life experiences.

2. Responsible assessment uses empirically-based, professionally sound, clinically relevant instruments and methods and a decision-making process that carefully considers and integrates information before drawing any conclusions.

3. Ethical assessment considers internal and external factors that influence the individual seeking counselling services (e.g., age, gender, health, disability, language, race, culture, family, and religion) –as well as the potential risks of assessment processes and instruments –and adheres to current professional practice standards and ethics.

4. Effective assessment helps to inform intervention, and continues during the counselling process in order to evaluate therapeutic response and effectiveness.”

Studying and learning implementing different types of assessments in our summer institute was the most beneficial. We learned how to conduct different assessments on anxiety, depression, PTSD, eating disorders, parenting, career transitions, relationships and personality assessments. My partner and I conducted our research on the Beck Anxiety Inventory (BAI). My partner and I are both teachers and have an interest in studying anxiety as it is one of the most common mental health concerns in schools.

Below are discussion board excerpts from EDPS 616 that showcase diversity, culture and family in assessment

Week 3 Question 1:

“No one client will ever be the same. That is the beauty of diversity. Clients will come from intersecting identities that make up the uniqueness of who they are. Balkin et al., (2014) urged counsellors not to generalize clients and clump them into categories based on their various intersections as this will limit and misinterpret the assessment process. To appreciate and capture clients’ diversities I will assess and counsel clients that is consistent to their cultural values and beliefs, and attempt to understand their worldviews and assumptions (Sue & Sue, 2016). Urbina (2014) argued that even assessments that have been cautiously constructed still are vulnerable and prone to misconstruction which is why counsellors must be aware of their biases and judgements in the assessment process and sway clients in the process.

I think by using a collaborative, client-centered approach that this would result in client’s sharing their diversity and more positive outcome for client’s in the assessment process. Acevedo-Polakovich et al, (2007) outlined four ways that assessments are conducted in a culturally sensitive and specific way, such as counsellors receiving formal and assessment training, maintaining this training throughout their practice, and knowing the client’s history and encompasses them, individually, and through their communities. My hope is that I make my client’s comfortable enough to share their stories and that I am sensitive to all matters of diversity without accidently offending anyone or making assumptions based on my own views.”

Week 3 Question 2:

“Taking a holistic approach into the consideration of cultural contexts such as socioeconomic status, race, and ethnicity can help reduce harmful and neglectful aspects of assessment practices (Hays, 2017). I really enjoyed reading through the tip sheet on multicultural assessment on pages 94 and 95 in the Hays (2017) text. One point discussed argued that exploring your own bias and assumptions about cultural groups are necessary as we need to reflect on how assumptions you hold influence how you assess clients. Sue and Sue (2016) state that culturally competent counsellors attempt to understand their own worldviews, biases, and values, as well as their client’s worldviews and history of oppression that they may have faced. In addition, Hays (2017) states that collaborating with clients, other professionals, consulting test manuals and other assessment materials, and keeping up to date with research and ethical practices are necessary to reduce neglectful aspects of assessment practices. In accordance with the Canadian Code of Ethics for Psychologists, it states that engaging in regular monitoring, assessment, collaboration and reporting promotes the development of knowledge, care and responsibility to society and individuals (CPA, 2017). The concept of contextual assessment is critical as it is necessary to look at all the contexts and intersections in a client’s life, including, individually and throughout their community. The more we understand client’s worldviews and explore biases and assumptions about cultural groups in terms of race, ethnicity, gender, ect. assessment practices will become less harmful and neglectful.”

References: Canadian Psychological Association. (2017). Canadian code of ethics for psychologists (4th ed.). Ottawa, ON: Author. Hays, D. G. (2017). Assessment in counseling: Procedures and practices (6th ed.). Alexandria, VA: American Counseling Association. Sue, D. W., & Sue, D. (2016). Counseling the culturally diverse: Theory and practice (7thed.). Hoboken, New Jersey: John Wiley & Sons, Inc.
