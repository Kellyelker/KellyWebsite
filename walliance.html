Working Alliance

In my opinion, the working alliance is arguably the most important aspect of counselling. Reflecting through this journey the past three years, I believe that regardless of your theoretical orientation the best predictor of change and growth is developing a working alliance between you and your counsellor. From personal and professional experience in counselling, developing a rapport and bond with your counsellor will lead to client change and success.

Below is my first assignment developing and learning the working alliance skills. (posted with permission)

Assignment

Discussion Board Posts from EDPS 622 that showcase the importance of the Working Alliance

“There is not a doubt in my mind that the working alliance creates positive outcomes in therapy. From our first semester in fall to our third semester this spring, we have read an abundant amount of articles, journals, and books that argue that the therapeutic alliance is one of the strongest predicators of change. Truscott (2010) states that highly effective therapists establish a mutual understanding toward goals that are meaningful to the client and true collaboration comes from empathically listening to the clinics view of their place in the world. Truscott (2010) was the first book I read in this program. From this week’s readings we have read similar arguments. Horvath (2006) states that there is a strong amount of evidence that the therapeutic relationship is strongly linked to positive client change. This essential feature of therapy consistently proves that a stronger working alliance creates improved therapeutic outcomes. Safran and Muran (2006) argued that that concept of the therapeutic alliance “highlights the fact that at a fundamental level the patient’s ability to trust, hope and have faith in the therapists ability to help always plays a central role in the change process” (pp. 289). From my own personal experience in therapy I definitely agree that feeling the alliance, support, and trust between you and the therapist creates a positive experience which most likely will lead to positive change.”

Horvath, A. O. (2006). The alliance in context: Accomplishments, challenges, and future directions. Psychotherapy: Theory, Research, Practice, Training, 43(3), 258-263. DOI:10.1037/0033-3204.43.3.258

Safran, J. D., & Muran, J. C. (2006). Has the concept of the therapeutic alliance outlived its usefulness? Psychotherapy: Theory, Research, Practice, Training, 43(3), 286-291. DOI:10.1037/0033-3204.43.3.286

Truscott, D. (2010). Becoming an effective psychotherapist: Adopting a theory of psychotherapy that's right for you and your client. Washington, DC: American Psychological Association.

“One important counsellor variable and teacher as well (since I am both of these roles right now) that is important to the success of the working alliance is warmth. Young (2017) argues that warmth has a powerful effect on the client’s willingness to open Furthermore, Nissen-Lie, Monsen, & Ronnestad (2010) state that counsellors who are perceived as warm, accepting, and empathic establish a successful working alliance. Alliance ratings are considerably more positive when clients perceive their counsellor exhibiting these warm traits and feel as though the counsellor understands their emotional expressions and feelings (Nissen-Lie, et al., 2010). I definitely agree with these arguments as I have been teaching for 4 years now and taking a job as a mental health teacher combined the role of a counsellor/teacher. When students know that you sincerely care about them, want to understand how they are feeling, accept them, and are empathic to their situations the working alliance will continue to be strong and successful. Young (2017) demonstrates how nonverbal messages can communicate warmth, such as tone of voice, gestures, facial expression and eye contact. All of these traits are so important to facilitate because when students or clients recognize warmth they respond by opening up which builds trust in the relationship.”

Nissen-Lie, H. A., Monsen, J. T., & Ronnestad, M. H. (2010). Therapist predictors of early patient-rated working alliance: A multilevel approach. Psychotherapy Research, 20(6), 627-646. http://dx.doi.org/10.1080/10503307.2010.497633

Young, M. E. (2017). Learning the art of helping: Building blocks and techniques. Boston, MA: Pearson.

“Combining Keeney’s (1990) model with the working alliance structuring skills from Hiebert (2005) can be very useful to a counsellor as both models discuss the importance of meaningful focus on each counselling session. For example, Keeney (1990) discusses the importance of the overall flow of the counselling process, as each session (beginning, middle, and end) progresses through frames of meaning. This is very similar to Hiebert (2005) as he argued that when clients know what to expect and can identify where they are in the process of counselling, it will help create a more meaningful interaction between the counsellor and client. Being able to map out and understand the natural flow of the sessions will allow the counsellor to reflect and assess on whether the use of these skills develop and maintain the working alliance from the beginning to end.”

Hiebert, B. (2001). Creating a working alliance: Generic interpersonal skills and concepts. Calgary, AB: University of Calgary.

Keeney, B. P. (1990). A method of scoring conversation and therapy. In B. P. Keeney (Ed.), Improvisational therapy: A practical guide for creative clinical strategies (pp. 40- 53). New York, NY: The Guilford Press.
